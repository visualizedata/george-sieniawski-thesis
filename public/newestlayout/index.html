Since propositions correspond with facts, it is evident that when in future events there is a real alternative, and a potentiality in contrary directions, the corresponding affirmation and denial have the same character.”

Global seapower competition is intensifying as traffic patterns on the world’s maritime highways continue to shift. In addition to massive physical commodity flows, there is also a virtual dimension to this highly globalized process in the form of accelerating Internet traffic, information, currency, and other financial exchange data flowing through the world’s sprawling network of subsea communications cables. Together, both are creating intriguing security targets for unscrupulous non-state actors who also use the oceans for piracy, contraband, modern-day slavery, and narcotrafficking, as well as for states which sometimes act in brazen disregard of public international law. Equally significant to this process is the ecological domain, where accelerated globalization -- or what Zygmunt Bauman calls “liquid modernity” -- is having a strong adverse impact on various forms of sea life.

Now, with China creating some 3,200 acres of territory ex nihilo by pouring sand/concrete over coral reefs and thereupon emplacing sea missiles, radar facilities, landing strips, and protected piers astride both sides of the Philippine archipelago, and with Russia dispersing submarines more widely across the Mediterranean/Black Seas and strafing NATO vessels in the Baltic Sea with fighter jets, U.S. defense planners face increasingly intricate maritime challenges across multiple theaters/engagement spaces. Against this backdrop, the Obama and Trump national security teams have both seen fit to dispatch U.S. aircraft carriers to an increasing variety of high-stakes situations, particularly in the 3rd and 7th Fleet Areas of Responsibility (AORs), where several high-profile incidents have occurred in recent years. Beyond addressing near-term threats, there is also the matter of planning and budgeting for the military needs of future decades, or what the Pentagon calls net assessment.

In order to understand these complex geostrategic dynamics, this study combines two distinct data sets: one on U.S. carrier movements and the other on diplomatic tensions worldwide, both unclassified and open-source in nature. Drawing from the prior net assessment literature within the public domain, it then examines spatial, programmatic, thematic, and chronological patterns in the aggregate data set, draws inferences as to how officials in Washington, DC define and protect the national interest, and presents conclusions as to the future of U.S. carrier diplomacy/naval aviation.

When mapped at the most granular level, the scope and significance of freedom of navigation operations becomes more apparent.
